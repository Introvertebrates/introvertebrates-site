<h2>What was found</h2>

<p>

  This study tested just how much useful taxonomic information can still be pulled from morphology in a very look-alike tarantula lineage. The authors compared <em>Lasiocyano sazimai</em> with species of <em>Lasiodora</em> using a large morphometric dataset that combined linear measurements, geometric morphometrics, discriminant analyses, and cross-validation.

</p>

<p>

  Some analyses did recover statistically significant differences, but the overall picture was messy: broad overlap between taxa, strong within-species variation, and overfitting that made the classifications much less reliable when tested properly. In practice, morphology alone did not provide dependable diagnostic separation between <em>Lasiocyano sazimai</em> and the <em>Lasiodora</em> species examined.

</p>



<h2>What makes that interesting</h2>

<p>

  Taxonomy often depends heavily on morphological characters, but this paper is useful because it directly tests the point where morphology stops being enough. Instead of assuming that more measurements automatically solve the problem, the authors show that some lineages are simply too conservative in form for shape-based identification to work cleanly on its own.

</p>

<p>

  One of the clearest takeaways is that only a small number of traits remained consistently informative at the generic level, including stridulatory setae in <em>Lasiodora</em> and the distinctive blue-purple setae of <em>Lasiocyano</em>.

</p>



<h2>Why it matters</h2>

<p>

  This matters because it sharpens how people think about spider taxonomy. A species or genus can be biologically real without being easy to separate using body form alone, especially in tarantulas where close relatives may look frustratingly similar.

</p>

<p>

  It also supports a broader lesson that applies well beyond this one lineage: integrative taxonomy is not academic overkill. In some groups, it is the only realistic way to avoid forcing neat names onto blurry morphological boundaries.

</p>
