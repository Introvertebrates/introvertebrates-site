<h2>What was found</h2>
<p>
  The study focused on jumping spiders from Western India and identified two species that were new to science. Both belong to the genus <em>Mogrus</em>, a group of salticids associated with dry and open habitats.
</p>
<p>
  Alongside the two new species, the work also expanded knowledge of already known spiders by documenting <em>Mogrus larisae</em> in India for the first time and formally describing the male of <em>Mogrus rajasthanensis</em>.
</p>

<h2>Why it matters</h2>
<p>
  New species discoveries are a reminder that spider diversity is still far from fully documented, even in places affected by urban pressure and human-altered landscapes. Research like this helps fill in the real picture of local biodiversity instead of the one people assume is already complete.
</p>
<p>
  It also shows that careful taxonomic work still matters. Naming new species is not just paperwork — it is the foundation for understanding distribution, ecology, and conservation.
</p>
